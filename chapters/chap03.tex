\section{Adversarial instance}

For a given $\delta > 0$, we define a coeficient $\alpha_i$ as $\sqrt{2}^i
+ \delta (2 + \sqrt{2})^i$.

We will construct an adversarial strategy, that forces any deterministic
algorithm $\A$ to be at least $\sqrt{2}$-competitive.

We can always ignore all but the heaviest pending packet from step $t - 2$ and
all but two heaviest packets from step $t - 1$, as only those can be scheduled.

The adversary begins the instance by releasing two packets of weight $\alpha_0$
with span $2$ and one packet of weight $\alpha_1$ with span $3$. At time $t$ we
distinquish two cases:
 \begin{enumerate}
    \item $\A$ schedules the heaviest packet at time $t$.
    \item $\A$ schedules one of the lighter packets at time $t$.
\end{enumerate}

In case $1$, the adversary terminates the sequence as shown in Figure
\ref{img:general-end-stopping}. In case $2$, the adversary continues the
sequence by releasing two packets of weight $\alpha_t$ with span $2$ and one
packet of weight $\alpha_{t+1}$ with span $3$.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:alg-behaviour}
Any reasonable algorithm $\A$ never schedules the lightest pending packet before
the adversary terminates the sequence.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
We show, that if algorithm $\A$ schedules the lightest packet at time $t$, then
it could have scheduled a heavier packet, which would increase its gain.

Since the instance did not terminate after step $t$, the algorithm must have
exactly $3$ pending packets of weigh $\alpha_t$, which can be scheduled in the
next two steps. Therefore at least one of those packets can not be scheduled by $\A$. \xxx{TODO}
\end{proof}

After sufficiently many steps, the adversary can terminate the instance as shown in Figure

\section{Lowerbound}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:general-infinite}
Assume that the algorithm $\A$ never schedules the heaviest packet. Then, for all
$\varepsilon > 0$ and $\delta > 0$, there exists $k_0$ such that terminating the
general instance after $k_0$ steps yields a competitive ratio of at least
$\sqrt{2} - \varepsilon$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}

Assume that the adversary terminates the instance after $k$ steps as shown in
Figure~\ref{img:general-end-infinite}.

By Lemma~\ref{lem:alg-behaviour}, at every step $t < k$ the algorithm $\A$
schedules a packet of weight $\alpha_t$ with span $2$. At step $k$, $\A$ has
three pending packets of weight $\alpha_k$ expiring at step $k + 2$ and two
pending packets of weight $\alpha_{k - 1}$ expiring at step $k + 1$. Therefore, it
can schedule packets of total weight at most $2\alpha_k$.

The maximum gain of algorithm $\A$ is:

$$
W_{\A} = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \alpha_i + \alpha_{k}
$$

When we take the limit of $\frac{W_\A}{(\sqrt{2} + 2)^k}$ as $k$ approaches infinity,
everything except for terms with $(\sqrt{2} + 2)^k$ and $(\sqrt{2} + 2)^{k+1}$
vanishes, yielding:

\begin{equation}\label{INF:eq1}
\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{W_\A}{(\sqrt{2} + 2)^k} =
\delta \left( \frac{\sqrt{2} + 2}{\sqrt{2} + 1} + 1 \right)
\end{equation}

The optimal algorithm schedules all packets with span $3$ and the last two
packets of weight $\alpha_{k}$ with span $2$, as shown in Figures
\ref{img:general-start} and \ref{img:general-end-infinite}. This results in a
gain of:

$$
W_{\OPT} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \alpha_i + 2\alpha_k
$$

Again, when we take the limit of $\frac{W_\OPT}{(\sqrt{2} + 2)^k}$ as $k$ approaches
infinity, most of the terms vanish, yielding:

\begin{equation}\label{INF:eq2}
\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{W_\OPT}{(\sqrt{2} + 2)^k} =
\delta \left( \frac{\sqrt{2} + 2}{\sqrt{2} + 1} + 2 \right)
\end{equation}

Combining Equations \ref{INF:eq1} and \ref{INF:eq2}, we compute the limit of the
competitive ratio:

$$
\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} =
\frac{\frac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2} + 1} + 2}{\frac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2} + 1} + 1}
= 1 + \frac{1}{\frac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2} + 1} + 1} = \sqrt{2}
$$

Therefore, for all $\varepsilon > 0$ and $\delta > 0$ there exists $k_0$ such that terminating
the general instance after $k_0$ steps yields a competitive ratio of at least
$\sqrt{2} - \varepsilon$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:general-finite}
If the algorithm $\A$ ever schedules the heaviest packet, then its competitive ratio
is at least $\sqrt{2} - \varepsilon$, for every $\varepsilon > 0$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $k$ be the step at which $\A$ schedules the heaviest packet.

As in Lemma~\ref{lem:general-infinite}, for each step $t < k$, the algorithm $\A$
schedules a packet of weight $\alpha_t$ with span $2$. At step $k$, it schedules
the heaviest packet of weight $\alpha_k$. After step $k$, $\A$ has three pending
packets of weight $\alpha_{k-1}$ with deadline $k + 2$. Hence, it can schedule at
most one of these packets before they expire. This results in a gain of:

$$
W_\A = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \alpha_i
$$

The optimal algorithm schedules the heaviest packet released at each step $t < k$
and all packets released at step $k$, resulting in a gain of:

$$
W_\OPT = \sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i + 2\alpha_{k-1}
$$

We compute the competitive ratio:

$$
\frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i + 2\alpha_{k-1}}{\sum_{i=0}^{k} \alpha_i} =
1 + \frac{2\alpha_{k-1} - \alpha_0}{\sum_{i=0}^{k} \alpha_i}
$$

Substituting the definition of $\alpha_i$ yields:

$$
\frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} = 1 + \frac{2 \sqrt{2}^{k-1} - 1 + \delta (2 (2 +
\sqrt{2}^{k-1})}{\frac{\sqrt{2}^{k+1} - 1}{\sqrt{2} - 1} + \delta \frac{(2 +
\sqrt{2})^{k+1} - 1}{1 + \sqrt{2}}}
$$

Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:fraction}, we obtain:
$$
\frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} \geq 1 + \min\left(\sqrt{2} - 1, \frac{2 (2 + \sqrt{2})^{k-1} - 1}{\frac{(2+\sqrt{2})^{k+1} - 1}{1 + \sqrt{2}}}\right)
$$

Consider the second term. As $k$ approaches infinity:

$$
\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{2 (2 + \sqrt{2})^{k-1} - 1}{\frac{(2+\sqrt{2})^{k+1} - 1}{1 + \sqrt{2}}}
= \frac{2 + 2\sqrt{2}}{(2 + \sqrt{2})^2} = \sqrt{2} - 1
$$

Therefore there exists $k_0$ such that for all $k \geq k_0$,
$\frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} \geq \sqrt{2} - \varepsilon$.

Now we choose $\delta$ such that $\delta (2 + \sqrt{2})^{k_0}$ is negligible
compared to $\sqrt{2}^{k_0}$. More formally, for any $k < k_0$, we take the
limit as $\delta$ approaches $0$:

$$
\lim_{\delta \to 0} \frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} =
1 + \frac{2\sqrt{2}^{k-1} - 1}{\frac{\sqrt{2}^{k+1} - 1}{\sqrt{2} - 1}}
= \sqrt{2}
$$

Therefore there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\frac{W_\OPT}{W_\A} \geq \sqrt{2} -
\varepsilon$ for all $k < k_0$. This completes the proof.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}\label{lem:fraction}
For positive real numbers $a, b, c, d$ it holds that:

$$
\frac{a + b}{c + d} \geq \min\left(\frac{a}{c}, \frac{b}{d}\right)
$$
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $m = \min \left(\frac{a}{c}, \frac{b}{d}\right)$. Using the fact that $c$
and $d$ are positive we have:

\begin{gather*}
c m \leq a \\
d m \leq b
\end{gather*}

Summing these inequalities gives us the desired inequality.
\end{proof}
